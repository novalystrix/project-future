## 1. What Already Happened — The Software Market

### The Canary in the Coal Mine

Software development was the first white-collar profession to feel the full force of AI displacement, and the data through early 2026 tells a stark story.

**Developer Salaries and Hiring**

While top-tier compensation at elite firms (trading firms, FAANG) has remained high — Hudson River Trading still pays $410K+ for entry-level, Netflix $325K for mid-level (Levels.fyi 2024 report) — these numbers mask what's happening in the broader market:

- **Total tech layoffs**: By early 2025, layoffs.fyi tracked 27,470+ tech employees laid off across 42 companies in 2025 alone, continuing the trend from 2022-2024 where over 400,000 tech workers were let go.
- **Entry-level collapse**: Junior developer positions have been decimated. Companies increasingly use AI coding assistants (Copilot, Cursor, Claude, Devin) to amplify senior developers, eliminating the need for junior staff. Job postings requiring 0-2 years of experience dropped approximately 40-50% from 2022 peaks.
- **Median salaries declining**: Outside the top 20 companies, median software engineer compensation in the US dropped 10-15% from 2022 highs. The "rest of the market" that Levels.fyi doesn't showcase has been hit much harder.
- **H-1B and global competition**: AI tools have equalized the playing field for offshore developers. A developer in India or Eastern Europe using Claude or GPT-4 now produces output comparable to a more expensive US-based junior, accelerating offshoring-via-AI.

**Freelance Markets: Upwork and Fiverr**

The freelance platforms tell the most dramatic story:

- **Upwork** reported declining revenue growth and a shift in project types. Simple web development, WordPress sites, basic mobile apps — the bread and butter of freelance developers — have been largely absorbed by AI. Clients can now generate these themselves.
- **Fiverr** saw the bottom fall out of its programming and tech categories. Logo design, copywriting, basic coding gigs — the sub-$500 project market — has been devastated. Fiverr's stock price fell from its 2021 high of ~$336 to under $30 by late 2024, a 90%+ decline.
- **Freelance rates** for standard web development dropped 30-50% as supply massively outstripped demand. Many freelancers pivoted to "AI integration" services, but this too became commoditized quickly.

**Coding Bootcamp Enrollment**

The bootcamp industry has been gutted:

- Lambda School (now BloomTech) filed for bankruptcy proceedings in 2024.
- General Assembly, Flatiron School, and others reported enrollment drops of 30-60% from peak.
- The value proposition — "learn to code in 12 weeks, get a $100K job" — collapsed as the jobs those graduates targeted were the first to be automated.
- University CS enrollment, while still strong at elite institutions, began declining at mid-tier schools starting 2024-2025.

**Impact on Tech Company Valuations**

The market bifurcated sharply:

- **AI winners**: NVIDIA (peaked at ~$3.5T market cap), Microsoft, Google, Meta, and pure-play AI companies saw massive valuation increases.
- **AI losers**: SaaS companies without AI moats saw severe compression. Traditional IT services firms (Infosys, Wipro, Cognizant, Accenture) faced existential questions about their labor-arbitrage business models.
- **The "picks and shovels" paradox**: Companies selling AI infrastructure thrived, while companies whose products AI could replace — website builders, basic analytics tools, customer support platforms — saw valuations crater.

The software market's experience is the template for what's coming to every other knowledge-worker profession. It happened here first because the tools were built by people who understood the domain.

## 2. Historical Parallels — Deep Research

### 2.1 The Great Depression (1929-1939)

**Chain of Events:**
- 1920s: Massive speculative bubble in stocks, fueled by margin lending (10:1 leverage common). New technologies (radio, automobiles, electrification) drove irrational exuberance.
- October 1929: Stock market crash. Dow fell 25% in two days. But the crash alone didn't cause the Depression.
- 1930-1933: Bank failures cascaded. 9,000+ banks failed. The Fed tightened money supply (the critical policy error). Smoot-Hawley tariffs collapsed global trade by 65%.
- 1933-1937: Partial recovery under FDR's New Deal.
- 1937-1938: "Roosevelt Recession" — premature austerity caused a relapse.
- 1939+: WWII finally ended the Depression through massive government spending.

**What held value**: Farmland (you could eat), gold (until FDR confiscated it in 1933), government bonds (deflation made fixed payments more valuable), cash (deflation increased purchasing power), essential businesses (food, utilities).

**What collapsed**: Stocks (-89% peak to trough), real estate (-30-50% in cities), commodities (farm prices dropped 60%), luxury goods, speculative assets.

**What recovered**: Everything eventually, but it took 25 years for the Dow to return to its 1929 peak (1954).

**Key lesson**: In a deflationary collapse, cash and high-quality bonds are king. Debt is deadly.

### 2.2 The 2008 Financial Crisis

**Chain of Events:**
- 2000-2006: Housing bubble fueled by subprime lending, securitization (MBS, CDOs), and rating agency failures.
- 2007: Subprime mortgage defaults began. Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed.
- March 2008: Bear Stearns sold to JPMorgan for $2/share (down from $170).
- September 2008: Lehman Brothers bankruptcy. AIG bailout ($185B). Merrill Lynch sold to Bank of America. Money markets "broke the buck."
- 2008-2009: Global credit freeze. Stock markets fell 50%+. Unemployment hit 10%. US home prices fell 33% nationally, 50%+ in bubble markets.
- 2009-2013: Slow recovery via QE (money printing), ZIRP (zero interest rates), bank bailouts (TARP).

**What held value**: US Treasuries (flight to safety), gold (rose from $700 to $1,900 by 2011), cash, high-quality corporate bonds, farmland.

**What collapsed**: Real estate (-33% average, -50%+ in Florida/Nevada/Arizona), bank stocks (-90%+), corporate bonds (temporarily), oil (from $147 to $30), equities broadly.

**What recovered**: Stocks recovered by 2013. Real estate took until 2016-2020 depending on market. Banks that survived became bigger than ever.

**Key lesson**: The government will bail out the system — but slowly and unevenly. Being liquid during the panic lets you buy assets at generational lows.

### 2.3 The Dot-Com Bust (2000-2002)

**Chain of Events:**
- 1995-2000: Internet mania. NASDAQ rose from 1,000 to 5,048. Companies with no revenue went public at billion-dollar valuations.
- March 2000: NASDAQ peaked. The trigger was a combination of rising interest rates, insider selling, and the realization that "eyeballs" didn't equal profits.
- 2000-2002: NASDAQ fell 78%. $5 trillion in market value destroyed. Pets.com, Webvan, eToys — hundreds of companies went bankrupt.
- Tech employment collapsed. Silicon Valley office vacancy hit 25%+.

**What held value**: Profitable tech companies (Microsoft, eBay, Amazon survived), real estate (barely affected outside Silicon Valley), bonds, value stocks.

**What collapsed**: Speculative tech stocks (-78% to -100%), telecom (WorldCom, Global Crossing bankrupt), venture capital dried up.

**What recovered**: Amazon went from $107 to $7, then to $3,000+ over 20 years. The internet itself was never the problem — the valuations were.

**Key lesson**: Technology revolutions are real, but the speculative excess around them destroys wealth. The technology survives; most investors in it don't.

### 2.4 Industrial Revolution Job Displacement (1780-1850)

**Chain of Events:**
- Mechanized textile production displaced millions of hand weavers and spinners. A handloom weaver earned 23 shillings/week in 1805; by 1833, it was 6 shillings.
- Luddite riots (1811-1816): Workers destroyed machinery. The government deployed 14,000 troops — more than Wellington had in the Peninsular War.
- Agricultural enclosure acts displaced peasant farmers from common land.
- Urbanization created squalid factory towns. Life expectancy in industrial Manchester was 17 years for working class.
- 1830-1850: Real wages finally began rising as new industries absorbed displaced workers.

**The timeline**: It took 60-80 years for the average worker to benefit from industrialization. An entire generation suffered.

**Key lesson**: Technology transitions ARE net positive — eventually. But "eventually" can mean decades of misery for those displaced. The relevant question isn't "will new jobs be created?" but "what happens in the gap?"

### 2.5 Japan's Lost Decades (1990-2020s)

**Chain of Events:**
- 1980s: Massive real estate and stock market bubble. The Imperial Palace grounds were "worth more than California." Nikkei hit 38,957 in December 1989.
- 1990: Bubble burst. Nikkei fell 60% by 1992. Real estate began a 15-year decline of 70-80%.
- 1990-2003: Banking crisis. Banks held massive bad real estate loans but refused to write them down ("zombie banks"). Government repeatedly bailed them out.
- Deflation set in. Consumer prices fell for 15+ consecutive years.
- Government ran massive fiscal deficits. Debt-to-GDP reached 250%+.
- Interest rates went to zero, then negative. Nothing worked.

**What held value**: Cash and government bonds (deflation made them more valuable), gold (somewhat), foreign assets (yen depreciation helped eventually).

**What collapsed**: Real estate (-70-80%), stocks (-80%, took 34 years to recover — Nikkei only reclaimed its 1989 high in 2024), bank stocks, consumer confidence.

**Key lesson**: Asset deflation can last DECADES. Japan's experience is the closest parallel to an AI-driven deflation scenario. When an economy's growth engine stalls, traditional monetary and fiscal policy can fail completely.

### 2.6 Weimar Republic Hyperinflation (1921-1923)

**Chain of Events:**
- WWI reparations + war debt + loss of industrial capacity created unsustainable fiscal position.
- Government printed money to pay obligations. Mark went from 4.2 per dollar (1914) to 4.2 trillion per dollar (November 1923).
- Middle class was wiped out. Life savings became worthless. A loaf of bread cost 200 billion marks.
- Those with hard assets (land, foreign currency, gold) survived or thrived.
- Resolution: New currency (Rentenmark) backed by real estate, plus Dawes Plan restructuring.

**What held value**: Real estate, gold, foreign currencies (dollar, Swiss franc), productive businesses, farmland.

**What collapsed**: Cash, bonds, fixed-income investments, pensions, savings accounts — anything denominated in the inflating currency.

**Key lesson**: When governments resort to extreme money printing, hold ANYTHING except the currency. This scenario becomes relevant if governments respond to AI unemployment with massive money-printing for UBI.

## 3. The AI Collapse Simulation — Chain of Events (2025-2035)

### Phase 1: Knowledge Worker Displacement (2025-2027)

**What's already happening and accelerating:**

- **Programming**: AI agents (Claude, GPT-5, Gemini, Devin, Cursor) can now produce production-quality code, debug, deploy, and maintain systems. By 2027, a single senior engineer with AI tools does the work of a 10-person team. Companies that employed 500 engineers now need 50-100.
- **Legal**: AI handles contract review, legal research, document drafting, due diligence, and regulatory compliance. Paralegals and junior associates are eliminated first. Major law firms reduce headcount 30-50%.
- **Accounting & Finance**: AI handles bookkeeping, tax preparation, financial analysis, audit procedures, and compliance reporting. The Big 4 begin massive restructuring.
- **Marketing & Advertising**: AI generates copy, designs ads, manages campaigns, optimizes spending, creates video content. Marketing departments shrink 60-70%.
- **Design**: AI generates UI/UX, graphic design, product design, architecture renderings. Photoshop/Figma workflows become prompt-based.
- **Journalism & Content**: Already largely displaced. AI generates articles, reports, summaries at near-zero marginal cost.
- **Consulting**: McKinsey, BCG, Bain — their core product (smart people analyzing data and writing PowerPoints) is precisely what AI excels at.

**Quantification**: These sectors employ roughly 30-40 million people in the US alone. If 50% are displaced over 3 years, that's 15-20 million high-income job losses.

**First-order effects:**
- These are $75K-$300K/year earners. Their spending powers the upper-middle-class economy: restaurants, luxury goods, real estate, cars, travel, private schools.
- Tax revenue begins declining. These workers pay disproportionate income taxes.
- 401(k) contributions drop. Stock market selling pressure increases as displaced workers liquidate retirement accounts.

### Phase 2: Service Sector Automation (2027-2029)

- **Customer service**: Already largely automated. Call centers, chat support — 90%+ handled by AI.
- **Sales**: AI handles lead generation, qualification, follow-up, CRM management, proposal generation. Inside sales teams eliminated. Field sales reduced.
- **Management**: Middle management — the coordinator class — becomes redundant when AI can track projects, allocate resources, monitor performance, and generate reports. Organizational structures flatten dramatically.
- **Education**: AI tutoring outperforms average teachers. Online education scales infinitely. Teaching jobs at all levels face pressure.
- **Healthcare administration**: Billing, coding, scheduling, insurance processing — all automatable. Clinical roles persist longer but face AI diagnostic assistance that reduces the number of physicians needed.

**Quantification**: Service sector is ~80% of US employment. Even 20-30% displacement means 25-40 million additional jobs at risk.

### Phase 3: Cascade Effects (2028-2030)

**Consumer spending collapse:**
- 35-60 million displaced workers (across Phases 1 and 2) dramatically cut spending.
- Even employed workers cut spending due to fear and uncertainty ("precautionary saving").
- Consumer spending is 70% of US GDP. A 15-20% decline triggers recession mechanics.

**Loan defaults begin:**
- Car loans: Displaced workers can't make payments. Auto loan defaults spike. Used car prices crash.
- Student loans: The already-stressed $1.7 trillion student loan market faces mass defaults as the degrees (law, MBA, computer science) that justified the debt no longer lead to jobs.
- Credit cards: Default rates climb from ~3% to 8-12%.
- Small business loans: Businesses that served knowledge workers (upscale restaurants, boutiques, services) fail.

**Deflationary pressure:**
- Reduced demand → falling prices for discretionary goods.
- AI dramatically reduces production costs for digital goods and services to near zero.
- But basic necessities (food, energy, housing) don't deflate as quickly, creating a "split inflation" dynamic.

### Phase 4: Banking System Stress (2029-2031)

**Commercial Real Estate Collapse:**
- Office occupancy was already at 50-60% post-COVID. With knowledge workers eliminated, it drops to 20-30%.
- US commercial real estate is a $20 trillion market with $5.8 trillion in debt. Office values in major cities drop 50-70%.
- Banks holding CRE loans face massive losses. Regional banks are most exposed.
- Retail real estate follows: malls, shopping centers, restaurants in business districts — all fail.

**Residential Mortgage Crisis:**
- Displaced workers default on mortgages. Default rates rise from ~1% to 5-10%.
- But unlike 2008, the problem isn't bad lending — it's genuine income loss. This makes it harder to resolve.
- Home prices in tech-heavy metros (SF, Seattle, Austin, NYC) drop 30-50%.
- Suburban/exurban "Zoom town" premiums from COVID era evaporate.

**Bank Failures:**
- Regional and mid-size banks fail first (echo of SVB/First Republic in 2023).
- FDIC insurance fund ($128B as of 2024) is insufficient for systemic crisis.
- Interbank lending freezes. Credit crunch begins.
- FDIC covers $250K per depositor per bank. Wealthy individuals and businesses with larger balances face real risk.

### Phase 5: Government Response (2030-2033)

**UBI Attempts:**
- Political pressure forces emergency UBI programs. Initial proposals: $1,000-2,000/month per adult.
- Cost: $3-4 trillion/year for a meaningful program. Current federal revenue is ~$4.4 trillion.
- The math doesn't work: the tax base that funded government is collapsing at the same time spending needs to increase.

**Money Printing:**
- The Fed resorts to massive QE — $5-10 trillion+ in asset purchases.
- MMT (Modern Monetary Theory) becomes de facto policy: print money, spend it directly.
- Government debt-to-GDP surges past 200%.

**Tax Base Collapse:**
- Income tax revenue falls as employment falls. Income tax is ~50% of federal revenue.
- Corporate taxes: AI-driven companies are highly profitable but employ few people. They also aggressively use tax optimization (IP in Ireland, etc.).
- Property tax revenue falls with real estate values, hitting local governments hardest. Police, fire, schools face budget crises.

**Political instability:**
- Populist movements surge. "Tax the robots" legislation. Wealth taxes. Windfall profit taxes on AI companies.
- International coordination fails — countries compete to attract the few remaining AI-economy jobs.
- Social unrest in economically devastated regions.

### Phase 6: Currency and Inflation Dynamics (2031-2035)

**The Great Paradox: Deflation vs. Inflation**

Two massive forces collide:

1. **Deflationary force**: AI collapses the cost of producing digital goods and services. Knowledge work goes from expensive to free. Software, design, legal services, content — all approach zero marginal cost. Demand collapse further depresses prices.

2. **Inflationary force**: Government prints trillions to fund UBI and bailouts. Monetary base expands 3-5x. Dollar purchasing power erodes.

**Most likely resolution: Stagflation with a twist**
- Asset prices and wages deflate (in real terms).
- Consumer goods prices inflate (due to money printing).
- Essential goods (food, energy) inflate most.
- Digital/knowledge goods deflate to near-zero.
- The result: a cost-of-living crisis despite "deflation" in headline GDP.

**What happens to each asset class:**

| Asset | Direction | Reasoning |
|-------|-----------|-----------|
| **Residential Real Estate** | ↓ 20-50% (varies by market) | Income loss → defaults → forced selling. But physical shelter retains floor value. |
| **Commercial Real Estate** | ↓ 50-80% | Offices and retail in structural decline. Some converts to residential/data centers. |
| **US Stocks (broad market)** | ↓ 30-50%, then bifurcates | AI companies surge; everything else collapses. S&P becomes "The Magnificent 7 and Friends." |
| **Bonds (US Treasuries)** | Initially ↑, then ↓ | Flight to safety initially. Then inflation fears and debt levels cause selloff. |
| **Gold** | ↑ 100-300% | Classic crisis hedge. Central bank buying accelerates. $5,000-8,000/oz plausible. |
| **Bitcoin/Crypto** | Volatile, eventually ↑ | Sells off in liquidity crunch. Then benefits from currency debasement narrative. |
| **Oil** | ↓ 30-50% initially, then stabilizes | Demand destruction from economic collapse. But supply constraints persist. |
| **Food/Agriculture** | ↑ 50-200% | Money printing + supply chain disruption + growing demand from developing world. Farmland value soars. |
| **Commodities (broad)** | Mixed | Industrial metals down (construction slows), precious metals up, energy volatile. |

## 4. What Will Still Have Value?

### Physical Assets

**Farmland and Agriculture**
- The single most resilient asset in every historical collapse. People always need to eat.
- US farmland has returned ~11% annually for 50 years with minimal volatility.
- Currently prices ~$5,000/acre (national average), but quality farmland in Iowa/Illinois is $10,000-15,000+/acre.
- AI can't disrupt the fundamental need for arable land, water, and sunlight.
- Risk: Climate change alters which land is productive.

**Water Rights**
- In the western US, water rights are increasingly the most valuable component of land ownership.
- "Whiskey is for drinking; water is for fighting over." — Mark Twain (attributed).
- Companies like Cadiz Inc. and water-rights-holding agricultural companies represent a way to play this theme.
- Water scarcity will intensify regardless of AI dynamics.

**Energy Infrastructure**
- Power generation and distribution remain essential. AI actually INCREASES energy demand dramatically (data centers).
- Utilities with regulated returns provide stable income.
- Solar, wind, and nuclear infrastructure investments benefit from both energy transition and AI power demand.
- Natural gas infrastructure retains value as a bridge fuel.

**Essential Commodities**
- Food, energy, water, basic materials — anything with inelastic demand retains value.
- Copper, lithium, uranium — materials needed for the AI/energy buildout.

### Gold and Precious Metals

Gold has been a store of value for 5,000 years. In every scenario analyzed in Section 2:
- Depression deflation → gold held value (until confiscated).
- 2008 crisis → gold went from $700 to $1,900.
- Hyperinflation → gold preserved purchasing power perfectly.
- Japan's deflation → gold in yen terms performed well.

Current gold price (~$2,900/oz in early 2026) reflects growing monetary concerns. Central banks (China, India, Turkey, Poland) have been buying aggressively — over 1,000 tonnes/year.

**Risks**: Government confiscation (happened in 1933 US). Storage and insurance costs. No yield.

### Bitcoin and Cryptocurrency

**The bull case**: Digital gold. Fixed supply (21M coins). Seizure-resistant. Borderless. Benefits from currency debasement.

**The bear case**: Correlates with risk assets in panics (fell 75% in 2022, fell with stocks in 2020). Regulatory risk. Energy consumption. Could be banned.

**Realistic assessment**: Bitcoin at ~$90,000-100,000 (early 2026) has established institutional legitimacy (ETFs, corporate treasuries). In a currency crisis scenario, it likely performs well. In a deflationary crisis, it initially sells off hard before recovering. A 5-10% portfolio allocation is reasonable as "collapse insurance."

### Skills and Human-Only Services

What AI cannot replace (yet):
- Physical trades: Plumbing, electrical, HVAC, construction. Robots are far behind AI in physical manipulation.
- Healthcare: Surgery, physical therapy, nursing — hands-on care.
- Human connection: Therapy, coaching, eldercare, childcare.
- High-stakes negotiation and leadership.
- Creative vision and taste (directing, curating, not generating).
- Local, relationship-based businesses.

## 5. The Banking System Collapse Scenario

### How Fractional Reserve Banking Fails

Banks operate on fractional reserves: they hold ~10% of deposits and lend out the rest. This works when:
1. Most depositors don't withdraw simultaneously.
2. Most borrowers repay their loans.
3. Asset values (collateral) remain stable.

An AI displacement crisis attacks all three pillars simultaneously:

**Pillar 1 — Deposit flight**: Unemployed workers draw down savings. Businesses reduce cash balances. Deposits shrink.

**Pillar 2 — Loan defaults**: Unemployed borrowers can't repay. Default rates on consumer loans could hit 10-15% (vs. normal 2-3%). Mortgage defaults 5-10% (vs. normal ~1%).

**Pillar 3 — Collateral collapse**: Real estate values fall 30-50%. The loans are suddenly larger than the assets backing them (negative equity). Banks' capital ratios deteriorate.

### Commercial Real Estate: The $5.8 Trillion Time Bomb

- US CRE debt outstanding: ~$5.8 trillion.
- Banks hold ~$2.7 trillion of this directly.
- Office vacancy rates already 20%+ in major cities pre-AI displacement.
- With knowledge workers gone, office demand could fall 50-70%. Office values drop accordingly.
- Regional banks have 4.4x the CRE exposure (as % of assets) compared to large banks.
- Scenario: 200-400 regional bank failures over 2-3 years (vs. 465 during the 2008-2013 period).

### Residential Mortgages

- Total US mortgage debt: ~$13 trillion.
- If unemployment reaches 15-25% (vs. current ~4%), mortgage defaults could affect $1-2 trillion in loans.
- Unlike 2008, borrowers post-2010 generally have better credit and more equity. This provides a buffer.
- But if home prices fall 30-40%, even well-qualified borrowers face negative equity and strategic default incentives.

### Bank Bail-ins vs. Bail-outs

**2008 model (bail-out)**: Government injects capital into banks. Taxpayers bear the cost. Banks survive.

**Post-2008 regulations**: Dodd-Frank introduced "bail-in" provisions. In a crisis, bank creditors (including large depositors above $250K) can be forced to absorb losses. This already happened in Cyprus (2013) where deposits above €100K were haircut 47.5%.

**In the AI scenario:**
- Political appetite for bailing out banks will be LOW when millions are unemployed.
- Bail-in provisions may be activated, meaning deposits above $250K are at risk.
- FDIC insurance fund (~$128B) can cover small-bank failures but not a systemic crisis.
- The Fed would likely backstop the system eventually (as it always does), but the interim period could be chaotic.

### Central Bank Responses

The Fed's toolkit:
1. Cut interest rates to zero (already done in 2008, 2020).
2. QE: Buy government bonds and mortgage-backed securities.
3. Emergency lending facilities (discount window, etc.).
4. NEW: Potentially buy corporate bonds, equities (already crossed this line in 2020).
5. NOVEL: Direct digital currency distribution (digital dollar / CBDC) — enables helicopter money directly to citizens.

The European Central Bank, Bank of Japan, and Bank of England would face the same pressures simultaneously, meaning there's no "safe haven" currency — all major currencies debase together.

## 6. Actionable Strategy: What Should I Do With My Money NOW?

### Framework: The Barbell Strategy for AI Uncertainty

The honest answer is: nobody knows which scenario plays out. The smart approach is a barbell strategy — protect against catastrophic downside while maintaining exposure to massive AI upside.

### Recommended Allocation for Significant Capital ($1M+)

**Tier 1: Collapse Protection (30-40%)**

| Allocation | Asset | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| 10-15% | Physical gold (coins/bars in secure vault) | Ultimate crisis hedge. 5,000-year track record. |
| 5-10% | Bitcoin (self-custody) | Digital hedge against currency debasement. |
| 5-10% | Farmland / agricultural REITs | Income-producing, inflation-resistant. |
| 5% | Cash in multiple currencies (USD, CHF, SGD) | Liquidity for opportunities. Geographic diversification. |
| 5% | Short-term US Treasuries / I-bonds | Safe, liquid, inflation-protected (I-bonds). |

**Tier 2: AI Upside Exposure (30-40%)**

| Allocation | Asset | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| 15-20% | AI infrastructure stocks (NVIDIA, TSMC, energy/data center companies) | Picks-and-shovels play. Wins regardless of which AI company dominates. |
| 5-10% | AI application layer (selective — companies building AI agents, not just using AI) | Higher risk, higher reward. |
| 5-10% | Energy stocks/infrastructure (uranium, natural gas, utilities) | AI needs power. Structural demand increase. |

**Tier 3: Opportunistic Cash (20-30%)**

| Allocation | Asset | Rationale |
|-----------|-------|-----------|
| 20-30% | Cash / money market / short-term bonds | Dry powder to buy assets at crisis prices. In 2008, those with cash bought real estate at 50 cents on the dollar. |

### Debt Strategy

**Is debt good or bad in this scenario?**

It depends entirely on the inflation/deflation outcome:

- **If deflation wins**: Debt is DEADLY. Your obligation stays the same while asset values and income fall. This destroyed borrowers in the 1930s and Japan.
- **If inflation wins (money printing)**: Debt is a GIFT. You repay with cheaper dollars. This is what happened to anyone with a mortgage during 1970s inflation.
- **The hedge**: Maintain moderate, fixed-rate, long-term debt on productive real assets. Pay off variable-rate and short-term debt. Don't lever up speculatively, but don't rush to be debt-free either.

**Specific guidance**:
- Fixed-rate mortgage at 3-4% on your primary residence? KEEP IT. This is valuable optionality.
- Variable-rate debt? Refinance to fixed immediately.
- Margin loans / business lines of credit? Reduce exposure. These get called in crises.

### Geographic Diversification

- Don't keep all assets in one country or currency.
- Consider: Singapore (stable, business-friendly, multicurrency), Switzerland (neutral, strong institutions), select Middle Eastern states (sovereign wealth provides stability).
- Physical presence or residence permits in 2+ countries provides personal optionality.
- Real estate in countries with strong property rights and diverse economies.

### Business Positioning

For a tech entrepreneur specifically:

1. **Build AI-native businesses**: Don't compete against AI; build on it. Companies with 5 people and AI that do what 500-person companies used to do.
2. **Own the customer relationship**: AI commoditizes production; distribution and trust become more valuable.
3. **Target AI-proof sectors**: Healthcare, physical services, government/defense, regulated industries (they adopt slowly).
4. **Revenue model**: Recurring revenue from essential services. Not project-based work that AI can undercut.
5. **Stay lean**: In uncertain times, the company that can survive on $0 revenue for 18 months wins.

## 7. Wild Cards and Alternative Scenarios

### Government Intervention Scenarios

**Robot Tax / AI Tax:**
- Bill Gates proposed this in 2017. Several EU countries have explored it.
- Mechanism: Tax companies on the "labor equivalent" their AI displaces.
- Problem: Defining and measuring displacement. International coordination (companies relocate to avoid it).
- Likelihood: Some version passes in major economies by 2028-2030.

**Wealth Tax:**
- Concentrated AI-driven wealth creates political pressure for wealth taxes.
- Elizabeth Warren's proposal: 2% on wealth above $50M, 3% above $1B.
- Constitutional challenges in the US. Easier in Europe.
- Effect: Accelerates capital flight to favorable jurisdictions.

**Universal Basic Income:**
- Sam Altman's Worldcoin/World Network and OpenAI have pushed UBI narratives.
- Finland, Kenya, and various US cities have run pilots.
- Scale is the challenge: meaningful UBI ($2K/month) costs $6+ trillion/year in the US.
- Likely outcome: Phased implementation, starting with expanded unemployment insurance, then targeted supplements, eventually broad UBI funded by money creation.

### New Economy Emergence

The optimistic case — and it's not negligible:

- **Abundance economics**: When AI makes knowledge work free, the cost of starting businesses drops to near zero. Millions of micro-entrepreneurs emerge.
- **New job categories**: Just as the internet created jobs nobody imagined in 1995 (social media manager, UX designer, cloud architect), AI may create roles we can't foresee.
- **Creative explosion**: When production is free, taste, curation, and creative direction become the scarce resources.
- **Science acceleration**: AI accelerates drug discovery, materials science, energy research. Breakthroughs create new industries.
- **Post-scarcity goods**: Software, content, design, education — these become effectively free. Standard of living for consumers rises dramatically.

### Best Case vs. Worst Case

**Best Case (20-30% probability):**
- AI displacement happens gradually over 15-20 years.
- New industries absorb displaced workers (as happened with every past technology shift).
- Governments implement effective retraining + safety nets.
- AI-driven productivity growth raises living standards broadly.
- GDP grows faster than jobs are lost.
- Result: Mild recession, then a golden age of abundance.

**Worst Case (10-20% probability):**
- Displacement happens in 5-7 years — too fast for adaptation.
- Political systems fail to respond effectively (paralysis, populism, conflict).
- Banking system crisis triggers financial contagion.
- Currency crises in multiple countries.
- Social unrest, political extremism.
- Result: A decade-long depression worse than the 1930s, with some countries experiencing Weimar-style currency collapse.

**Most Likely Case (50-60% probability):**
- Severe disruption over 7-12 years.
- Multiple recessions.
- Significant government intervention (UBI-like programs, AI taxes, massive spending).
- Eventual adaptation, but a "lost decade" for many workers and asset classes.
- Extreme wealth concentration in AI-owning class.
- Resembles Japan's experience but faster, with eventual resolution.
- Result: Painful but survivable. The prepared do well; the unprepared are devastated.

### What If AI Creates MORE Wealth Than It Destroys?

This is the Goldman Sachs view: generative AI raises global GDP by 7% ($7 trillion). The argument:

- AI is a tool, like electricity. It augments human productivity rather than replacing humans entirely.
- Companies that adopt AI become more competitive, grow faster, hire differently but not fewer.
- The cost reduction from AI flows to consumers as lower prices, increasing real purchasing power.
- New capabilities (AI doctors in developing countries, AI tutors for every child) create massive value.

**The counterargument**: Electricity didn't do the actual work of lawyers, programmers, and accountants. It powered machines that still needed human operators. AI IS the worker. The analogy breaks down precisely because AI targets cognitive work — the work that humans were supposed to retreat to when machines took the physical jobs.

**The synthesis**: AI almost certainly creates more total wealth. The question is distribution. If AI-generated wealth concentrates in the hands of AI-company shareholders while 40% of the workforce is displaced, total GDP might grow while median living standards collapse. This is the most likely failure mode — not insufficient wealth creation, but catastrophically unequal distribution.

## Conclusion: What to Do Right Now

1. **Don't panic, but don't be complacent.** The disruption is real and accelerating. You have 2-5 years before the most severe effects.

2. **Build your barbell.** Protect the downside (gold, farmland, cash, geographic diversification) while maintaining upside (AI infrastructure investments, AI-native businesses).

3. **Stay liquid.** Cash is ammunition. The best investments in history were made during crises by people who had liquidity when others didn't.

4. **Reduce fragility.** Fixed-rate debt only. Multiple income streams. Skills that AI can't replace. Relationships in multiple jurisdictions.

5. **Build, don't just invest.** As a tech entrepreneur, your highest-return move is building AI-native companies. The winners of the next decade will be small teams leveraging AI to do what large companies used to do. Capital + AI expertise + execution speed = massive advantage.

6. **Watch the signals.** Bank stress indicators (CDS spreads, deposit flows). Commercial real estate defaults. Government bond yields. Unemployment claims. When these start moving together, the cascade is beginning.

The AI revolution is the most significant economic disruption since industrialization. Unlike industrialization, it's happening in years, not decades. The playbook from history is clear: stay liquid, own real assets, diversify geographically, and be positioned to buy when others are forced to sell.
