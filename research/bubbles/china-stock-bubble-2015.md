# China Stock Market Bubble (2014–2016)

> **The most government-encouraged bubble in modern history** — where state media promoted equity investment as patriotic duty, margin lending was extended to retail investors with little income or experience, and when the crash came, the government's failed intervention became as legendary as the bubble itself.

---

## Overview

China's stock market bubble of 2014–2015 was distinct from virtually all other historical bubbles because **the government actively created it** through official media encouraging equity investment as part of national economic strategy, then desperately tried to stop it from falling once it turned. The Shanghai Composite rose 150% in 12 months (June 2014 to June 2015), then fell 45% in three months, then continued falling. Tens of millions of Chinese retail investors — many of whom had borrowed heavily to invest — lost fortunes.

The China 2015 bubble is also instructive because it demonstrates that even a state with near-complete economic control cannot prevent or control asset bubbles once they have formed. Despite spending hundreds of billions of dollars in market support, banning short-selling, halting trading in half the market's shares, and arresting people for "spreading rumors" of price falls, the market continued falling.

## Historical Context

### China's Economy (2010–2014)
- Post-2008 Global Financial Crisis: China launched a massive ¥4 trillion stimulus ($586 billion) that prevented recession but created enormous overcapacity in steel, cement, real estate
- GDP growth slowing: from 14% (2007) to 7.4% (2014)
- Real estate bubble peaking in 2013–2014: developers overbuilt, prices plateauing
- Chinese leadership (under Xi Jinping from 2013) wanted to rebalance economy from investment to consumption
- **Key policy goal**: Develop domestic capital markets to reduce reliance on bank lending as corporate financing mechanism

### The Government's Bull Market

- **Late 2014**: State media begins promoting equity markets as a "healthy bull market"
- *People's Daily* (official party newspaper) runs multiple articles encouraging ordinary citizens to invest in stocks as a patriotic act and as a path to shared prosperity
- This was not subtle: official media was the mechanism for creating the bubble
- China Securities Regulatory Commission (CSRC) dramatically expanded margin lending availability
- Margin lending requirements relaxed: brokerages allowed to lend up to 3x invested capital
- "Shadow margin" through unregulated channels offered 5–10x leverage to retail investors
- Chinese retail investors historically treated the stock market as gambling — "speculation" rather than investment — but government endorsement changed this

### The Retail Investor Phenomenon
- **June 2015 peak statistic**: 90 million individual retail investors were active in Chinese markets — more than the entire membership of the Chinese Communist Party
- Many were first-time investors: farmers, factory workers, retirees attracted by official encouragement and tales of easy money
- Financial literacy: surveys showed large percentages of new investors had never graduated high school
- Mobile trading apps made it trivially easy to open margin accounts
- Account openings: ~4 million new accounts per week at peak of mania (June 2015)

## Timeline: Formation to Crash

### Phase 1: Foundation (Mid-2014)
- Shanghai Composite at ~2,000 (July 2014) — had been stagnant for years
- Both Shanghai (A-shares) and Shenzhen (smaller companies, more tech-heavy) begin rising
- Driven initially by institutional investors and property money rotating into equities

### Phase 2: Official Endorsement and Acceleration (Late 2014)
- *People's Daily* publishes "Bull market has just begun" (November 2014)
- State TV runs programs explaining stock investing to the masses
- CSRC announces expanding margin lending
- Shanghai Composite breaks 3,000 (December 2014) — up 50% from July

### Phase 3: Mass Participation Mania (January–June 2015)
- Wave after wave of retail investors enter market
- By April 2015: Account openings at record pace; street vendors, farmers, taxi drivers all "investing"
- Classic late-stage indicators:
  - Shoeshine boys, hairdressers, construction workers discussing specific stock picks
  - Companies changing names to include "internet" or "tech" keywords to boost share prices
  - Multiple stocks hitting daily 10% limit up (maximum daily gain allowed under circuit breakers)

**Shanghai Composite Performance:**

| Date | Level | Change |
|------|-------|--------|
| July 2014 | 2,049 | Baseline |
| December 2014 | 3,234 | +58% |
| March 2015 | 3,696 | +80% |
| May 2015 | 4,900 | +139% |
| June 12, 2015 (peak) | 5,166 | +152% |

**Shenzhen Composite** (smaller, tech-heavy): Rose even more dramatically — from ~1,000 (July 2014) to ~3,000+ (June 2015) — nearly 3x

**Valuation at Peak:**
- Shanghai Composite P/E ratio: ~26x (elevated but not insane for benchmark)
- Shenzhen Composite P/E ratio: ~60–70x (clearly bubble territory)
- ChiNext (startup companies board): P/E ratio of ~130x at peak
- Many individual stocks: 200–500x earnings or no earnings at all

### Phase 4: The Crash Begins (June–August 2015)

**June 12, 2015**: Shanghai peaks at 5,166

**June 15–19**: Selling begins; CSRC announces margin lending rules will be tightened — immediately seen as removing the fuel driving the market

**June 26**: Shanghai falls 7.4% in a single day — one of the largest single-day drops in the index's history

**Initial Response**: State-owned enterprises ordered to not sell shares; pension funds deployed to buy

**July 8, 2015**: Market circuit breakers triggered; approximately 1,400 companies (49% of all listed companies) suspend trading — preventing any price discovery in half the market

**By late August 2015**: Shanghai Composite at ~2,900 — down 44% from peak in 10 weeks

## The Government Intervention: A Study in Failed Control

The Chinese government's response to the crash is one of the most dramatic and ultimately unsuccessful market interventions in history:

### The National Team (国家队)
- A consortium of state entities dubbed the "National Team" was deployed to purchase equities:
  - China Securities Finance Corporation (CSF): State entity with unlimited central bank credit
  - Central Huijin Investment: State investment arm
  - Multiple state-owned brokerages buying on behalf of the government
  - Pension funds ordered to deploy capital into equities
- **Estimated spending**: ¥1.5–2.5 trillion ($240–400 billion) over several months

### Trading Restrictions
- **Short selling**: Effectively banned; margin calls restricted
- **IPOs suspended**: New share issuances halted
- **Circuit breakers**: 10% daily move limit; stocks hitting limit-down immediately suspended
- **Lock-up periods**: Shareholders with >5% stakes banned from selling for 6 months
- **Trading halts**: Over 1,400 companies (representing ~40% of market cap) halted trading simultaneously

### Criminal Prosecutions
- Multiple people arrested for "spreading false information" that caused the market to fall
- A journalist at *Caijing* magazine arrested after reporting that the government was considering withdrawing market support
- Foreign investors and banks investigated for "malicious short selling"
- The state attempted to make the market fall itself illegal

### Why It Failed
Despite these extreme measures, the market continued falling because:
1. **Margin debt was enormous**: Estimated ¥2.2 trillion in official margin + unknown "shadow margin" — forced selling was automatic when prices fell
2. **Scale mismatch**: ¥1.5 trillion in government support vs. ¥2.2 trillion in forced margin selling
3. **Suspended stocks were a trap**: Half the market suspended = those stocks could not be sold, but when they reopened, selling cascaded
4. **Confidence destroyed**: Each government intervention revealed the government was worried, which made investors MORE worried

### The Second Crash (August 2015 — "Black Monday")
- August 24, 2015: Global markets hit by renewed Chinese selling fears
- Shanghai fell 8.5% — "Black Monday" for global markets
- S&P 500 fell 3.9%; Dow Jones fell 588 points (single-day point record at the time)
- Germany's DAX fell 5.6%
- This was among the first major examples of Chinese financial instability creating global contagion

## Who Got Wiped Out vs. Survived

### Wiped Out
- **Leveraged retail investors**: The combination of margin loans (3x–10x) with 50% price declines was catastrophic — many owed more than they had invested
- **Farmers and workers who invested life savings**: Made financially illiterate investments based on state media encouragement
- **Companies that had borrowed against their own stock**: Founders of listed companies who had pledged shares as collateral for loans faced forced selling
- **Some smaller brokerages**: Exposed to defaults on margin loans

### Survived
- **Index investors who stayed long and waited**: Shanghai eventually recovered to ~3,500 range, though not to 2015 peak
- **Foreign institutional investors who got out early**: Had profit cushion from earlier gains
- **The "National Team"**: State entities were not marked-to-market the same way; their losses were ultimately socialized

## Economic Consequences

### Limited Macroeconomic Impact (Surprisingly)
- Unlike 2008's housing crash, China's stock market crash caused relatively limited GDP impact
- This is because Chinese households' equity wealth is smaller relative to their real estate wealth
- China's housing market (much larger wealth store) remained stable through 2015
- GDP growth: 6.9% in 2015 — slightly below target but within range
- The **transmission mechanism** from stock crash to consumer spending was weaker in China than it would be in the US

### However: Longer-Term Damage
- Chinese retail investor confidence in domestic equities destroyed
- The Shanghai market has underperformed global equities significantly since 2015
- Foreign institutional investors remain deeply skeptical of Chinese market regulatory environment
- The interventions to "stop" the crash created enormous legal uncertainty for market participants

### Global Contagion
- The August 2015 "Black Monday" demonstrated China's growing global financial influence
- Commodity prices (especially iron ore, copper) fell sharply as China growth fears spread
- Emerging market currencies sold off broadly
- This was the first major evidence that Chinese financial instability could export volatility globally

## Regulatory Response

### Immediate Failures
- The CSRC's interventions during the crisis were widely criticized as making things worse:
  - Mass trading suspensions prevented orderly price discovery
  - Arresting "market manipulators" (including short sellers) for legal activities
  - Forced government buying didn't address underlying leverage problem

### Post-Crash Reforms
- New volatility circuit breakers introduced (January 2016) — immediately triggered and SUSPENDED after two days (caused massive selling as everyone rushed to sell before trading halted)
- This is a perfect example of poorly designed regulation worsening a crisis
- Longer-term improvements to margin lending oversight
- Enhanced disclosure requirements for large shareholders

## Lessons

### What China 2015 Teaches That No Other Bubble Does

1. **Government creation AND government failure**: No other major bubble was both officially created by state encouragement AND resisted by state intervention. Both the creation and the failed rescue demonstrate that no government has unlimited control over markets.

2. **Leverage kills markets faster than sentiment**: The forced selling from margin calls overwhelmed even ¥2+ trillion in government support.

3. **Trading halts are not solutions**: Suspending 49% of the market just concentrated the selling pressure into the remaining 51%.

4. **Mass retail investor participation is a late-stage indicator**: When 90 million retail investors are active (equal to the CCP's membership), you are definitionally near the peak.

5. **Political legitimacy creates bubble legitimacy**: Because the government encouraged investment, retail investors assumed the government would protect them. This delayed the exit of late entrants who trusted official reassurances.

### The AI Parallel
China 2015 has a specific parallel to government responses to AI employment disruption:
- **Governments may try to "ban" or slow AI deployment** the way China tried to ban market falls
- **This will fail in the same way**: You cannot legislate away economic disruption caused by genuine productivity differences
- **The intervention may make things worse**: Delaying the adjustment creates more violent eventual adjustment
- **Mass retail investor protection**: Just as China's retail investors relied on government protection, workers displaced by AI will expect government protection — and the government's ability to provide it may be more limited than expected

## Other Significant Bubbles Worth Noting

### The Nifty Fifty Bubble (1970s US)
- 50 blue-chip US stocks (IBM, Xerox, Polaroid, etc.) were "one-decision stocks" — buy and hold forever
- P/E ratios of 50x–100x in 1972
- Crashed in 1973–1974 bear market: average Nifty Fifty fell 60–70%; some fell 90%+
- Xerox: from $171 (1972) to $49 (1974)
- Recovery: A decade; some companies never recovered their Nifty Fifty valuations

### The Conglomerate Bubble (1960s US)
- Companies that assembled unrelated businesses were valued at persistent premiums
- Litton Industries, Ling-Temco-Vought, Gulf+Western
- P/E ratios of 40–50x for diversification premium that was mostly financial engineering
- Collapsed when growth accounting games were exposed
- Led to massive restructuring/breakups in the 1970s–1980s

### The SaaS/Growth Bubble (2020–2022)
- Distinct from crypto but parallel
- Revenue multiples (P/S ratios) for high-growth SaaS companies reached 50–100x sales
- Fed rate hikes from 0.25% (early 2022) to 5.5% (2023) destroyed duration assets
- **Specific examples:**
  - Zoom: $588 peak (October 2020) → $65 (2023) = -89%
  - Shopify: $1,762 peak (November 2021) → $288 (October 2022) = -84%
  - Snowflake: $400+ peak → ~$120 (2023) = -70%
  - Peloton: $171 peak (January 2021) → $4 (2023) = -98%
- This was the direct precursor to the current SaaS market re-rating that the AI disruption thesis builds upon
