# US Housing Bubble & Global Financial Crisis (2002–2009)

> **The most complex financial collapse since 1929** — when the world's largest economy turned its housing stock into a casino, its mortgage-backed securities into weapons of mass financial destruction, and its bank leverage into a global contagion that nearly destroyed the international financial system.

---

## Overview

The 2008 Global Financial Crisis (GFC) was not simply a housing bubble — it was the housing bubble as the trigger for a systemic financial crisis that threatened the complete collapse of the global banking system. What distinguished 2008 from previous bubbles was not the underlying asset (housing) but the **financial engineering** that turned individual mortgages into complex securities, sold those securities to institutions worldwide, rated them incorrectly by ratings agencies, and levered the system to the point where a 20% fall in house prices could threaten the solvency of the world's largest banks.

The US housing bubble inflated from approximately 2002 to 2006, with national home prices rising ~87% in four years. The burst and subsequent financial crisis cost the global economy an estimated $15–22 trillion in lost wealth, caused the worst recession since the Great Depression, destroyed millions of jobs, and triggered government bailouts on a scale never previously attempted in peacetime.

## Historical Context

### Seeds: The Post-Dot-Com Environment
- **2001**: Fed begins cutting rates aggressively (from 6.5% to 1.75% in 11 months)
- **2003**: Fed Funds Rate hits 1% — the lowest since 1958
- Low rates made mortgages cheap and pushed investors seeking yield into mortgage-backed securities
- **Post-9/11 economic policy**: "Keep America buying" — consumer spending and housing as economic engine
- **Bush administration "Ownership Society"**: Policy push for increased homeownership, including relaxed lending standards
- The dot-com crash left investors burned on equities — "safe as houses" became the mantra

### Financial Innovation (1990s–2004)
The financial engineering that created the crisis built over two decades:

**Mortgage-Backed Securities (MBS)**: Mortgages bundled and sold as bonds — existed since the 1970s (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac), became increasingly complex
**Collateralized Debt Obligations (CDOs)**: MBS tranches rebundled into new securities — allowed AAA ratings on what were fundamentally risky subprime mortgages
**Synthetic CDOs**: CDOs that referenced mortgages via credit default swaps without actually owning mortgages — pure bets
**Credit Default Swaps (CDS)**: Insurance-like instruments on bond defaults; AIG sold billions without holding adequate reserves
**Asset-Backed Commercial Paper (ABCP)**: Short-term funding for long-term mortgage securities — created dangerous maturity mismatch

**The Rating Agency Failure:**
Moody's, S&P, and Fitch gave AAA ratings to CDO tranches that deserved junk ratings — because the models they used assumed national home prices could not fall simultaneously (they had never done so in post-WWII data). This was the fundamental analytical error.

## Timeline: Formation to Crisis

### Phase 1: Housing Boom (2002–2004)
- Low interest rates make mortgages affordable
- Housing prices begin rising 10–15% per year in hot markets
- Traditional 30-year fixed-rate mortgages remain dominant
- Subprime lending exists but is a fraction of total market

### Phase 2: Subprime Explosion (2004–2006)

**The critical shift**: When interest rates rose from 1% (2004) to 5.25% (2006), traditional buyers were priced out. The mortgage industry's response was to **lower lending standards**, not prices:

- **NINJA loans**: No Income, No Job, No Assets — loans to people with no ability to repay
- **Adjustable Rate Mortgages (ARMs)**: Low teaser rates for 2–3 years, then reset to much higher rates
- **"Liar loans"**: Stated income mortgages where borrowers self-reported income without verification
- **Piggyback loans**: 80/20 mortgages (80% first mortgage + 20% second mortgage = 100% financing + fees)
- **Interest-only mortgages**: Pay only interest for 5–10 years, then principal kicks in

**Subprime Mortgage Volume:**

| Year | Subprime Originations | % of Total Mortgages |
|------|----------------------|---------------------|
| 2001 | $160 billion | 8.6% |
| 2003 | $332 billion | 18.5% |
| 2005 | $625 billion | 21.2% |
| 2006 (peak) | $600 billion | 20.1% |

**The Originate-to-Distribute Model:**
The crucial change: mortgage originators (Countrywide, IndyMac, Washington Mutual) stopped holding loans — they originated them, packaged them into MBS, sold them to Wall Street, and kept the fees. They had **zero incentive to evaluate credit quality**.

### Phase 3: Housing Peak and Early Warning Signs (2006)
- **2006**: National home prices peak — S&P/Case-Shiller National Home Price Index at 184.6 (January 2000 = 100)
- Early subprime defaults begin rising
- **February 2007**: HSBC announces $10.6 billion in subprime losses — first major warning
- **April 2007**: New Century Financial (second-largest subprime originator) files bankruptcy
- Housing prices begin falling in weakest markets (Nevada, Florida, California, Arizona)

### Phase 4: Crisis Builds (2007)
- **June 2007**: Bear Stearns reveals two hedge funds with heavy subprime exposure are nearly worthless; bails them out for ~$3.2 billion (which was itself lost)
- **August 2007**: Credit markets seize; banks stop lending to each other (interbank lending freeze)
- **September 2007**: Northern Rock Bank in UK — first British bank run in 150 years; depositors queue in streets
- **October 2007**: Merrill Lynch discloses $8 billion in CDO losses; CEO Stan O'Neal fired
- **November 2007**: Citigroup discloses massive CDO exposure; CEO Chuck Prince fired
- Stock markets volatile but S&P 500 still near peak (October 2007 peak)

### Phase 5: Full Collapse (2008)

**Bear Stearns (March 2008):**
- Bear Stearns — the 5th-largest US investment bank — cannot roll over its short-term funding
- The Fed arranges an emergency purchase by JPMorgan Chase at $2/share (later raised to $10) — down from $160/share in 2007
- Federal Reserve provides $29 billion in guarantees — first use of emergency lending powers since the 1930s

**Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac (September 2008):**
- Both GSEs (Government-Sponsored Enterprises) — holding or guaranteeing $5.4 trillion in mortgages
- Treasury announces federal conservatorship — effectively nationalized
- September 7, 2008

**Lehman Brothers (September 15, 2008):**
- Fourth-largest US investment bank files for bankruptcy
- **$639 billion in assets** — the largest bankruptcy in US history (vs. WorldCom's $104 billion)
- The Fed and Treasury declined to arrange a rescue (unlike Bear Stearns)
- This decision remains controversial: Was it deliberate? Was it miscalculation?
- **Effect**: Immediate global financial freeze. Every major institution stopped trusting every other.

**AIG (September 16, 2008):**
- AIG — the world's largest insurance company — was selling credit default swaps (essentially insurance on CDOs) without holding adequate reserves
- When CDOs failed, AIG owed hundreds of billions it did not have
- Federal government provided $85 billion emergency credit facility (later grew to $182 billion total)
- AIG was "too interconnected to fail" — its failure would have triggered cascading failures at Goldman Sachs, Merrill Lynch, Deutsche Bank, and dozens more globally

**Washington Mutual (September 25, 2008):**
- Largest US S&L; $307 billion in assets
- Seized by FDIC; sold to JPMorgan Chase for $1.9 billion
- Largest bank failure in US history

**TARP and the Bailout:**
- **October 3, 2008**: Emergency Economic Stabilization Act passes — authorizes $700 billion TARP (Troubled Asset Relief Program)
- Initially intended to buy toxic assets; pivoted to direct bank capital injections
- **October 13, 2008**: Treasury Secretary Paulson summons 9 bank CEOs, tells them they are receiving government capital whether they want it or not:
  - Citigroup: $45 billion
  - Bank of America: $45 billion
  - JPMorgan Chase: $25 billion
  - Goldman Sachs: $10 billion
  - Morgan Stanley: $10 billion
  - (And others)

## The CDO/MBS Mechanism — Why It Was Different

Understanding WHY 2008 was different requires understanding the financial engineering:

**Step 1**: Countrywide makes 1,000 subprime mortgages totaling $200M. Instead of holding them, they package them into a Mortgage-Backed Security (MBS) and sell it to Goldman Sachs.

**Step 2**: Goldman Sachs takes 100 different MBS tranches — some AAA, some BBB — and repackages them into a Collateralized Debt Obligation (CDO). The CDO "diversification" allows 90% of the new security to be rated AAA even though the underlying mortgages are B-rated.

**Step 3**: Rating agencies (S&P, Moody's) apply historical models that have never seen nationwide house price declines — rate the CDO AAA.

**Step 4**: European pension funds, money market funds, insurance companies, sovereign wealth funds BUY the CDO because it yields slightly more than US Treasuries but is "AAA" safe.

**Step 5**: Simultaneously, hedge funds and banks create "synthetic CDOs" — CDOs that don't actually own mortgages but use Credit Default Swaps to REPLICATE the risk. This means the same mortgage pool can be referenced by 10x its actual size in synthetic instruments.

**Step 6**: AIG sells billions in CDS "insurance" on these CDOs to banks — without holding adequate capital.

**When house prices fall 20%:**
- The subprime mortgages start defaulting
- The CDO tranches start losing value (all at once, because "diversification" across geographies was false)
- The synthetic CDOs amplify losses 10x
- AIG faces hundreds of billions in CDS payments
- European institutions discover "AAA" CDOs are worth 20 cents on the dollar
- All of it happens simultaneously across the global financial system

The 2008 crisis was a **global credit event**, not merely a housing crash. The housing crash was the pin; the CDO/CDS structure was the balloon.

## Who Got Wiped Out vs. Who Survived

### Wiped Out
- **Shareholders of failed institutions**: Bear Stearns ($160 → $0.06), Lehman Brothers ($85 → 0), Washington Mutual ($47 → 0), Wachovia (sold for $7 from $55)
- **Homeowners in bubble markets**: Millions of Americans in Nevada, Florida, Arizona, California were "underwater" for years or lost homes to foreclosure
- **Pension funds worldwide**: European pension funds that bought "AAA" CDOs — Dutch, German, Irish, UK funds all suffered major losses
- **Iceland**: The entire country's banking system collapsed — three major banks failed with combined assets 10x Iceland's GDP. The three banks (Glitnir, Kaupthing, Landsbanki) were nationalized in October 2008.
- **Ireland**: Banking system required a government bailout that consumed 40% of GDP; required IMF/EU rescue
- **Greece, Spain, Portugal**: Exposed to the crisis through European bank holdings and their own real estate bubbles

### Who Survived and Profited
- **JPMorgan Chase (Jamie Dimon)**: Avoided the worst CDO exposure; bought Bear Stearns and WaMu for near-zero; emerged the dominant US bank
- **Wells Fargo**: Bought Wachovia for cheap; focused on retail banking, avoided the worst structured products
- **Goldman Sachs**: Infamously bet AGAINST mortgage securities in 2006–2007 (the "Big Short" trade) while selling them to clients; paid $550M settlement but was profitable through the crisis
- **John Paulson** (Paulson & Co.): Made ~$15 billion shorting subprime MBS in 2007 — the greatest single trade in history
- **Michael Burry** (Scion Capital): Made ~$100M personally on CDS on mortgage securities; immortalized in "The Big Short"
- **Steve Eisman, Kyle Bass, Andrew Lahde**: Other notable short sellers

### The Mechanics of the Big Short
- The "Big Short" trades used Credit Default Swaps (CDS) — essentially paying a small premium to insure against bond defaults
- Burry, Paulson, and others identified that subprime mortgages were far riskier than rated and would default massively
- They convinced Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, Bear Stearns to sell them CDS — the banks were happy to take the other side, believing the CDOs were safe
- When defaults came, the CDS holders collected hundreds of millions/billions per contract

## Economic Consequences

### The Great Recession (2007–2009)
- **US GDP contraction**: -5.1% (Q4 2008 through Q2 2009)
- This is the sharpest peacetime contraction since the Great Depression
- **Global GDP**: Fell 2.1% in 2009 — first global contraction since WWII
- **US unemployment**: Rose from 4.6% (2007) to 10.0% (October 2009)
- **Global trade**: Fell 12% in 2009 — sharpest decline since the 1930s
- **Housing starts**: Fell from 2.2 million/year (2006) to 478,000/year (2011)

### The Long Shadow
Even after the technical recession ended (June 2009), the recovery was slow:
- **"The New Normal"** (PIMCO's term): Slower growth, higher unemployment accepted as the new baseline
- Real wages stagnated for a decade
- Millennials entering the job market 2008–2012 suffered permanent wage scarring
- Household net worth didn't recover to 2007 levels until approximately 2013

### Banking System
- The US banking system was fundamentally transformed:
  - Pre-2008: 5 major investment banks (Goldman, Morgan Stanley, Merrill, Lehman, Bear)
  - Post-2008: 2 investment banks converted to commercial bank holding companies (Goldman, Morgan Stanley); Merrill absorbed into BofA; Lehman gone; Bear gone
  - Total commercial bank consolidation: Fewer, larger banks
- The largest US bank in 2024 (JPMorgan Chase: ~$3.4 trillion assets) is larger than entire US banking system was in the 1990s

### Mortgage Market Transformation
- Private label MBS essentially disappeared after 2008 and did not return at scale by 2026
- FHA, Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac now guarantee ~70% of all new mortgages (vs. ~50% pre-crisis)
- Down payment requirements restored; income verification mandatory
- 30-year fixed-rate mortgage rate volatility: spread between Treasuries and mortgages widened

## Government/Institutional Response

### The Bazooka: Federal Reserve
**Conventional tools (2007–2008):**
- Cut Fed Funds rate from 5.25% (September 2007) to 0–0.25% (December 2008)
- Opened numerous emergency lending facilities (TAF, PDCF, TSLF, AMLF, CPFF, MMIFF)

**Unconventional tools (2008–2014):**
- **Quantitative Easing (QE1, November 2008)**: Fed buys $600 billion in MBS from Fannie/Freddie — the Fed essentially becomes the mortgage market
- QE2 (November 2010): $600 billion Treasury purchases
- QE3 (September 2012): $40 billion/month MBS + $45 billion/month Treasuries — "open-ended"
- Fed balance sheet: $900 billion pre-crisis → $4.5 trillion (2014) → $8.9 trillion (2022)

### TARP and the Treasury
- $426 billion disbursed; $441 billion returned (including dividends and profits on AIG/GM)
- Net to taxpayers: approximately +$15 billion (excluding Fannie/Freddie losses)
- But: the moral hazard created is immeasurable — "too big to fail" became the expectation

### Dodd-Frank (July 2010)
The most comprehensive financial regulation since the Glass-Steagall Act (1933):
- **Volcker Rule**: Banned proprietary trading by banks with FDIC insurance
- **Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB)**: New agency to protect retail borrowers
- **FSOC (Financial Stability Oversight Council)**: To identify systemic risk
- **Stress tests (DFAST/CCAR)**: Annual bank stress tests to ensure capital adequacy
- **Derivative regulation**: Required most derivatives to be cleared through central counterparties
- **"Living Wills"**: Systemically important banks must file plans for their own orderly dissolution
- **Raised capital requirements (Basel III)**: Banks required to hold more and higher-quality capital

## Lessons for Modern Bubble Analysis

### What Made 2008 Uniquely Dangerous

1. **Financial engineering obscured risk** — the CDO/CDS structure made risk assessment nearly impossible for most participants; even the creators often didn't fully understand what they'd built

2. **Leverage upon leverage**: Investment banks were levered 30:1 (for every $30 of assets, only $1 of equity). A 3.5% decline in asset values wiped out equity entirely.

3. **Interconnection**: No major financial institution was truly isolated — AIG's failure would have directly caused Goldman, Merrill, Deutsche Bank to fail simultaneously

4. **Rating agency capture**: Rating agencies paid by the issuers being rated; perverse incentives created race-to-the-bottom in standards

5. **"Safe as houses" psychology**: An asset class that had never fallen nationally was assumed to be safe at any valuation

### What 2008 Tells Us About AI Economic Disruption

The 2008 pattern has a specific lesson for the AI displacement scenario:
- **The trigger asset (mortgages) was broadly distributed** — it was in pension funds in Norway, savings banks in Germany, insurance companies in Japan
- **Similarly**: if AI displaces employment sufficiently, the "distributed" asset that becomes toxic is consumer debt — mortgages, auto loans, credit cards held by people whose jobs no longer exist
- **The banking system is the transmission mechanism**: Just as 2008's housing losses became banking losses became global credit freeze, an AI employment shock would become consumer loan losses that become banking losses
- **Quantitative magnitude**: $16 trillion in US mortgage debt at the peak of 2008; US consumer + mortgage debt is now ~$20+ trillion; global household debt is ~$70 trillion
