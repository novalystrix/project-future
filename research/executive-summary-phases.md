# 🔴 Executive Summary: The AI Disruption Scenario

**Prepared for Roy · March 2026**

---

## The Core Thesis

We are living through the early stages of what may become the largest economic disruption since industrialization. AI systems — not theoretical future AI, but the tools available **today** (Claude, GPT-4o, Gemini, Devin, Cursor) — can already perform the core work of most knowledge jobs: writing, analysis, coding, legal research, financial modeling, customer service, marketing, design, and management coordination.

The question is not **whether** AI can replace these jobs. It can. The question is **when** the collective understanding of this becomes universal — and what happens to the economy when it does.

This document lays out the scenario in phases, the assumptions behind each, the signals to watch, and the actions to take depending on what we see happening.

---

## The Two Macro Scenarios

Everything below branches into two possible paths. Both start the same way but diverge at the critical moment:

### Scenario A: The Gradual Crush (More Likely — 50-60%)

AI displacement happens sector by sector over **7-12 years**. Companies adopt AI incrementally. Layoffs come in waves — first tech, then professional services, then broader white-collar. Each wave gives the economy time to partially absorb the shock before the next hits. Governments have time to react (imperfectly). Markets reprice slowly. It's painful but manageable — resembling Japan's lost decade more than a sudden crash.

**Key assumption:** Human psychology and corporate inertia slow adoption. Not every CEO moves at the same speed. Regulation, union resistance, and cultural resistance create friction.

### Scenario B: The Simultaneous Panic (Less Likely but Catastrophic — 25-35%)

A trigger event (viral demo, Fortune 500 mass layoff announcement, or a compound of signals in the same 2-week window) causes **universal, simultaneous understanding** that AI replaces most knowledge work. Unlike every prior financial panic, there is no debate about whether the threat is real — every knowledge worker can verify it personally in minutes by pasting their actual work into Claude or ChatGPT.

This eliminates the information-gathering phase that historically gave markets time to absorb shocks. The full price adjustment happens in **days, not months**. There is no buyer for the other side of the trade.

**Key assumption:** The technology is already good enough. The only barrier is psychological — and psychological barriers break all at once.

### Why Both Scenarios Lead to the Same Destination

Whether it takes 2 years or 10, the endpoint is the same: **30-60 million knowledge workers displaced**, consumer spending collapses, loan defaults cascade, banks face stress, governments print money, currencies debase. The difference is speed — and speed determines how much damage is done in the transition.

The phases below apply to both scenarios. In Scenario A, each phase takes 1-3 years. In Scenario B, phases 1-4 compress into months.

## The Assumptions

This entire analysis rests on a set of assumptions. If any are wrong, the scenario changes:

| # | Assumption | Confidence | What Breaks It |
|---|-----------|------------|----------------|
| 1 | AI can do the core work of most knowledge jobs TODAY | Very High | It already can. This is observable, not speculative. |
| 2 | Companies will adopt AI to cut costs | Very High | Capitalism. The first company to cut 30% of labor costs forces competitors to follow. |
| 3 | Displaced workers cannot easily transition to equivalent-paying jobs | High | Unlike prior tech revolutions, AI targets cognitive work — the "safe" work people were supposed to retreat to. |
| 4 | The speed of displacement exceeds the speed of job creation | High | New jobs will emerge, but retraining takes years. The gap is the danger. |
| 5 | Consumer spending collapse follows job losses | High | 70% of US GDP is consumer spending. Displaced + fearful workers spend less. |
| 6 | Loan defaults follow spending collapse | High | Mortgages, auto loans, credit cards — all depend on employment income. |
| 7 | Banking stress follows loan defaults | Medium-High | Banks are better capitalized than 2008, but commercial real estate exposure is massive. |
| 8 | Governments respond with money printing | High | They always do. The only question is scale. |
| 9 | Money printing leads to currency debasement | Medium-High | It did in every prior case. The USD's reserve status provides some buffer. |
| 10 | The transition eventually resolves (not permanent collapse) | High | New industries emerge. Humanity adapts. But "eventually" can mean a decade. |

**What could make this NOT happen:**
- AI development plateaus (possible but no signs of it)
- Governments implement effective preemptive policies (UBI, retraining) before crisis hits (historically unprecedented)
- New job categories emerge faster than old ones disappear (the optimistic case — possible, but betting on it is risky)
- Companies choose not to adopt AI (against all economic incentive — extremely unlikely)

The earnings improvement is real, but the **secondary effects** (consumer spending, tax revenue, housing demand) haven't hit yet. Don't be fooled by the earnings bump.

**Key signal:** When Big 4 firms, major law firms, or Fortune 500 companies announce AI-driven headcount plans with specific numbers — not vague "transformation" language — Phase 2 has begun.

**Consumer confidence collapses** even for those still employed. The "precautionary savings" effect kicks in — people who HAVE jobs spend less because they fear losing them.

### Phase 5: Consumer Spending Collapse (2029–2031)

**What happens:**
Displaced workers who haven't found equivalent jobs start cutting spending. Employed workers cut spending out of fear. Consumer spending is 70% of US GDP — a 3-5% decline means GDP contraction of 2-3.5%.

**The math:**
- Savings rate rises from 5-8% to 12-15% (precautionary)
- Every 1% rise in savings rate = ~$200B less spending
- A 7% rise = **$1.4 trillion annual spending reduction**
- Recession officially declared

**The cascade:**
1. Displaced worker cuts spending → local restaurant loses revenue → lays off staff → those workers' mortgages at risk → multiply by millions

**What collapses:** Travel, restaurants, new vehicles, home improvement, premium retail
**What holds:** Food staples, healthcare, utilities, housing payments (sticky — people pay until they can't)

**What to do:**
| Action | Why |
|--------|-----|
| 🟢 Buy long-duration US Treasuries | Fed cutting rates aggressively; this is THE bond trade |
| 🟢 Buy healthcare infrastructure | Stable demand, recession-resistant |

| 🔴 Sell growth stocks broadly | Growth requires spending; recession kills it |

### Phase 6: Banking System Stress (2030–2032)

**What happens:**
AI displacement stops being a labor story and becomes a **financial system story**. This is the 2008 parallel — not from mortgage fraud, but because employment is what supports all consumer credit.

**The credit time bomb:**
- US household debt: ~$18 trillion (mortgages, auto, student, credit cards)
- If 5% becomes non-performing: **$900 billion in defaults**
- Compare: 2008 had ~$1.1 trillion in subprime defaults that nearly collapsed global banking

**The doom loop:**
Worker loses job → stops paying mortgage → bank provisions for loss → bank earnings fall → bank capital weakened → bank reduces lending → less credit → less spending → deeper recession → more job losses → repeat

**Commercial real estate accelerant:** Office vacancy was already 20-25% post-COVID. With AI-reduced headcount, it drops to 30-40%+. Banks hold ~$2.7 trillion in CRE loans. Regional banks have 4.4x the CRE exposure of large banks — they fail first.

**What could prevent a 2008 repeat:** Better bank capitalization (Basel III), stricter mortgage underwriting post-2008, faster Fed response
**What could make it worse:** Fed already depleted rate-cutting ammunition, government debt already elevated ($35T+), structural unemployment can't be fixed by stimulus

### Phase 7: Government Response — Print and Pray (2031–2033)

**What happens:**
Governments deploy every tool they have:
- **Monetary:** Rates to zero, massive QE ($5-10T+), emergency bank facilities
- **Fiscal:** Extended unemployment benefits, retraining programs, infrastructure spending, UBI pilots
- **Radical:** "Robot tax" / AI tax on labor savings, wealth taxes, emergency legislation

**The Japan warning:** Japan launched 10 stimulus packages totaling ¥60 trillion between 1992-2000. It didn't work because the problem was structural. You can't stimulus-spend your way out of jobs that no longer exist.

**The inflation pivot:** Government spending + money printing = currency debasement. This is where the Weimar parallel kicks in. Not hyperinflation — but sustained, significant purchasing power erosion. Essential goods (food, energy) inflate most. Digital goods deflate to near-zero. Cost-of-living crisis despite "deflation" in headline numbers.

**Tax base collapse:** Income tax (50% of federal revenue) shrinks with employment. Property tax falls with real estate values. AI companies are profitable but employ few people and optimize taxes aggressively. The government needs MORE money at exactly the moment it has LESS.

### Phase 8: Bottom and Recovery — The Other Side (2033–2040)

**What happens:**
The economy restructures around the new reality. New job categories emerge (AI supervision, human-in-the-loop services, care economy, trades). Not enough to absorb all displaced workers quickly, but enough to stabilize.

**The shape depends on which recovery type:**
- **V/U-shaped (optimistic, 30%):** New AI-created industries absorb workers over 5-7 years. Productivity gains generate enough wealth to fund new consumption. Comparable to post-industrial revolution (but faster).
- **L/Japan-shaped (pessimistic, 40%):** New jobs require different skills. Retraining is slow. Wage compression is permanent for many. UBI becomes a permanent feature. A "lost generation" of workers.
- **Something in between (30%):** Severe but navigable. 5-10 year adjustment period. The prepared do well; the unprepared are devastated.

**The universal post-crisis trade (proven across every historical bubble):** Quality equities bought at the trough produce extraordinary returns. S&P 500 bought at the March 2009 low returned 178% by 2014. NASDAQ bought at the 2002 low returned 150% by 2007.
