# Banking and Credit System Impact: AI-Driven Job Displacement

> **When employment falls, credit fails** — a deep-dive analysis of how AI-driven labor displacement transmits through the banking and credit system, creating potential for a financial crisis comparable to or exceeding 2008.

---

## Introduction: Employment Is the Foundation of Credit

Every dollar of consumer credit — mortgage, auto loan, credit card, student loan, personal loan — is ultimately underwritten based on the borrower's ability to repay. And ability to repay is primarily a function of income.

The 2008 financial crisis was caused by a breakdown in the underwriting of mortgage credit: lenders made loans to people who couldn't afford them. The loans then went bad.

An AI-driven employment disruption would cause a **fundamentally different** but equally dangerous credit problem: **loans made to people who COULD afford them at time of origination** — when they had jobs — but can no longer afford them when those jobs are eliminated. This is not an underwriting failure. It is a structural economic change that invalidates the assumptions underlying trillions of dollars of existing credit.

## Mortgage Market: The Largest Single Risk

### The Stock of US Mortgages

- Total outstanding: ~$13 trillion
- Holder breakdown:
  - Agency MBS (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, Ginnie Mae guaranteed): ~$9 trillion (~70%)
  - Private label (bank balance sheets + non-agency MBS): ~$4 trillion (~30%)

### How Mortgage Defaults Cascade

**Step 1: The Individual Default**
- Worker loses job → income drops → cannot make $2,500/month mortgage payment
- Has savings: typically 3–6 months before default (if any savings)
- Goes 30 days late → 60 days late → 90 days late (serious delinquency)
- Bank files for foreclosure → process takes 6–18 months depending on state
- Bank puts house on market → excess supply depresses local prices

**Step 2: The Neighborhood Effect**
- Foreclosed home sells at distressed price (15–30% discount)
- Comps lower → all neighborhood values lower
- Adjacent homeowners with marginal equity now underwater
- Those homeowners: reduced spending (negative wealth effect) even without defaulting
- Some choose strategic default (owe more than worth; rational to walk away)

**Step 3: The Bank Effect**
- Bank holds a Mortgage: Goes from performing asset to non-performing
- Bank must increase reserves (provision for loan losses)
- Higher provisions reduce earnings → if large enough, reduce capital ratios
- Reduced capital ratio → bank must reduce new lending to maintain ratios
- Reduced lending = less consumer spending = deeper recession = more unemployment = more defaults

**This is the doom loop.** It self-reinforces until either:
(a) The government intervenes at scale (TARP 2008 model)
(b) Asset prices stabilize at a new lower equilibrium
(c) Inflation reduces real debt burden (requires significant monetary debasement)

### AI Disruption vs. 2008: The Key Differences in Mortgage Risk

**2008**: 
- Concentrated in subprime/alt-A loans (specific underwriting failure)
- Geographic concentration in Sun Belt bubble markets
- Primarily recent vintage (2004–2007 loans)
- Triggered by reset of adjustable rates + house price fall

**AI Disruption Scenario**:
- Affects PRIME borrowers — people who qualified properly but whose jobs are eliminated
- Geographic concentration in knowledge-worker cities (SF, Seattle, Boston, NYC) — higher median prices = higher absolute dollar losses
- Affects a wide vintage of loans (not just recent ones)
- Triggered by income loss, not rate reset

**Implication**: The 2008 crisis was more about BAD LOANS. An AI disruption crisis is about GOOD LOANS MADE BAD by structural change. The banking system is more directly exposed to prime borrowers (they hold more of those loans; they're on the "good" side of the credit spectrum) — which means the banks' own balance sheets are harder hit.

## Commercial Real Estate: The Other Shoe

### CRE Already in Stress Pre-AI

Commercial real estate (CRE) was already facing a structural crisis BEFORE AI is considered:

- **Office vacancy rate**: 20–25% nationally by 2025; rising in major markets post-COVID (hybrid/remote work)
- Office vacancy in San Francisco: 30%+ (2025)
- Office vacancy in New York financial district: 25%+
- **Value decline**: Prime office buildings in major cities off 30–50% from 2019 peaks
- **Refinancing cliff**: Enormous volume of 5-year CRE loans originated in 2018–2020 at 3–4% rates must be refinanced in 2023–2025 at 6–8% rates

**CRE loan maturity wall:**
- 2023–2027: Approximately $2.5 trillion in CRE loans mature and require refinancing
- Higher interest rates mean many cannot be refinanced at values supporting original loan size
- Property owners choosing to hand keys back to banks ("jingle mail" for office buildings)

### AI's Additional Impact on CRE

**Office Demand:**
- AI further reduces office demand by reducing headcount at office-dependent employers
- A company that deployed AI and cut 20% of knowledge workers needs 20% less office space
- Short leases turn over quickly; long leases lock in demand temporarily but expire
- By 2028–2030: additional 10–15% reduction in office demand from AI headcount cuts on top of existing vacancy

**Retail CRE:**
- AI-driven job displacement reduces consumer spending → less retail foot traffic → more retail vacancy
- AI-enabled e-commerce improvements further accelerate shift from physical retail

**Residential CRE (Apartments):**
- Initially: Displaced workers move from owned homes to renting (renting increases)
- Then: If unemployment extends, rent delinquencies increase
- In major markets: If tech/knowledge-worker exodus, apartment oversupply in those cities

### CRE Exposure in Banking System

Banks and financial institutions have significant CRE exposure:

| Institution Type | CRE Loan Exposure | Risk Level |
|-----------------|-------------------|-----------|
| Regional banks (assets $10–$100B) | Often 25–40% of loan book | 🔴 HIGH |
| Community banks (<$10B) | Often 30–50% of loan book | 🔴 HIGH |
| Large banks (JPM, BofA, Wells) | 15–25% of loan book | 🟡 MEDIUM |
| Insurance companies | Large CRE mortgage portfolios | 🟡 MEDIUM |
| REITs | Direct ownership; equity risk | 🔴 HIGH |

**The regional bank vulnerability:**
- Regional banks are often the most CRE-concentrated
- Smaller capital buffers to absorb losses
- Limited geographic diversification
- Silicon Valley Bank (2023) showed how quickly a bank run can destroy a regional bank
- The CRE stress + AI employment stress = compounded risk for regional banking sector

## Consumer Credit: Cards and Auto

### Credit Card Debt: Already Elevated

US credit card debt reached ~$1.1 trillion in 2024–2025 — record levels:
- Average household with credit card debt: ~$10,000 in credit card balance
- Average interest rate on carried balances: ~21–22% (post-rate-hike environment)
- Delinquency rates: Rising from COVID lows; already elevated for lower-income borrowers
- Charge-off rates: Rising at major banks

**AI displacement impact on credit cards:**
- Displaced workers who had $0 credit card balance (responsible borrowers) now USE credit cards to bridge income gap
- Bridge period: 3–6 months of credit card usage before acknowledging the job won't return
- Balance builds rapidly at 21–22% interest
- Then: Cannot service even minimum payments; default

**Scale calculation:**
- If 5% of US workers are displaced (approximately 8 million workers)
- Average credit card balance accumulation: $15,000–$25,000 before default
- Total credit card default potential: $120–200 billion
- Compare: All credit card charge-offs in 2009 (worst year of GFC): ~$80 billion
- This represents meaningful stress to credit card issuers (Synchrony, Ally, Capital One, Discover, American Express)

### Auto Loans: Subprime Already Stressed

Auto loan market characteristics:
- Total outstanding: ~$1.7 trillion
- Subprime auto loans (borrowers with credit score <640): ~$300–350 billion
- Subprime auto 90+ day delinquency: Already elevated at 3–4%+ (2025)
- **Key risk**: Auto loans are typically 5–7 year; underwater borrowers can't trade down without losses

**AI displacement impact on auto:**
- Workers who relied on cars for work (commuting, delivery) still need cars after displacement
- Workers who lose income and can't service auto loan: default; vehicle repossessed
- Surge in repossessed vehicles → used car prices fall → all auto loan collateral worth less
- Subprime auto lenders face default cascade

## Student Loans: The Hidden Bomb

$1.75 trillion in federal student loan debt has unique characteristics:

- **Federal government is the creditor** (not banks): Federal defaults don't directly hit bank balance sheets
- BUT: Delinquencies hurt borrowers' credit scores → makes them worse mortgage/credit card risks
- AND: Student loan payments (resuming after COVID pause) reduce disposable income → less consumer spending
- AND: Student debt prevents home purchase for many young workers → reduces housing demand

AI displacement is PARTICULARLY severe for recent graduates:
- Entry-level positions in law, finance, accounting, consulting, engineering — the fields for which people take on $100K+ in debt — are precisely the roles being eliminated or dramatically reduced
- A 2024 law school graduate paying $1,500/month in student loan payments who cannot find BigLaw employment faces a specific crisis

## Bank Runs: The Speed Risk

### The SVB Lesson (March 2023)

Silicon Valley Bank was solvent by most traditional measures in January 2023. By March 10, 2023, it was bankrupt. The trigger: about 48 hours of social media-accelerated bank run.

**The mechanism:**
- Rising interest rates reduced the value of SVB's long-duration bond portfolio (mark-to-market)
- SVB announced a capital raise to address the shortfall
- Announcement triggered fear → Twitter/X spread the concern → VCs called their portfolio companies → 42,000 customers withdrew $42 billion in ONE DAY
- FDIC closed the bank the next morning

**What this means for AI disruption:**
- Modern bank runs happen at the speed of social media
- There is no queuing in the streets (like Northern Rock 2007) or waiting for the weekend (like 2008 bank negotiations)
- A credible threat to a bank's solvency can translate to a closed bank in 24–48 hours
- The entire "too big to fail" framework assumes time to arrange rescues; that time may be measured in hours, not days

**Stage 6 bank run scenario:**
- News breaks: "Bank X has $XX billion in non-performing AI-related loans"
- Twitter/X spreads concern: "Should I be worried about Bank X deposits?"
- Bank's mobile app sees withdrawal spike
- Bank pauses withdrawals briefly to manage liquidity
- Pause itself triggers more fear (circular)
- Federal Reserve emergency discount window: backstop must be instant, credible, public

## FDIC Insurance: Limits and Limitations

### The Current State of Deposit Insurance

FDIC insures deposits up to **$250,000 per depositor per bank per account category**.

**Key account categories:**
- Single ownership: $250,000
- Joint accounts: $500,000 (two people × $250,000)
- Retirement accounts (IRAs): Separate $250,000 coverage
- Trust accounts: Up to $250,000 per beneficiary

**A household with two spouses can have:**
- Individual account (person 1): $250,000
- Individual account (person 2): $250,000
- Joint account: $500,000
- Person 1 IRA: $250,000
- Person 2 IRA: $250,000
- **Total insured at one bank**: $1.5 million

**For corporations and businesses**: Generally $250,000 per entity per bank, regardless of account count.

**The FDIC Fund:**
- Deposit Insurance Fund (DIF) balance: ~$120–130 billion (2025 estimate)
- Total insured deposits: ~$10+ trillion
- Coverage ratio: ~1.3%
- For comparison: In the 2008 crisis, the FDIC fund was nearly exhausted; the Treasury provided backstop
- **Bottom line**: The FDIC fund is a first line of defense. It is NOT sufficient for a systemic banking crisis. Federal backstop (Treasury, Fed) is the ultimate guarantee.

### What "Too Big To Fail" Means Now

The implicit guarantee:
- JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citigroup are systemically important
- Their failure would be catastrophic for the global financial system
- Government WILL bail them out in an existential crisis
- This is a moral hazard but it is the reality
- **However**: Shareholders and sometimes bondholders WILL be wiped out in any bailout (government takes equity)

**The regional bank risk:**
- There are ~4,000 FDIC-insured commercial banks in the US
- Most are NOT systemically important
- Many WILL be allowed to fail in a significant crisis
- This is where depositor risk is real for balances above FDIC limits

**Practical advice for individuals:**
1. Do not keep more than $250K at any single bank (per account category)
2. For business accounts: Keep operating cash spread across 2–3 major systemically important banks
3. For cash reserves above FDIC limits: Use T-bills directly (no bank counterparty risk)
4. Avoid depositing large sums in banks with concentrated CRE or consumer credit exposure

## The Debt-Deflation Spiral: The Macro Risk

If AI job displacement is sufficiently severe and rapid, the banking credit losses described above could trigger the debt-deflation spiral first described by Irving Fisher (1933) and later observed in Japan (1990–2005).

**The spiral mechanism:**

1. Workers lose jobs → can't service debt → default
2. Banks suffer losses → provision for losses → reduce lending
3. Reduced lending → less investment and consumption → GDP falls
4. GDP fall → more unemployment → more debt defaults
5. Banks recognize larger losses → reduce lending further
6. Asset prices (houses, commercial property) fall → collateral values fall → loans go underwater
7. Even performing borrowers reduce spending (negative wealth effect)
8. Lower prices make real value of debt HIGHER (deflation + debt = real debt growth)
9. This increases the burden on borrowers → more defaults → return to step 1

**The Japan example**:
Japan entered this spiral in the early-to-mid 1990s and couldn't exit it for 15 years despite:
- Near-zero interest rates (reached 0.5% by 1995)
- Massive fiscal stimulus programs
- Bank capital injections
- Low and falling inflation → deflation

The deflationary spiral is the worst-case outcome. It's not inevitable — but it requires:
- Rapid recognition of losses (not hiding bad loans)
- Aggressive monetary AND fiscal response
- Allowing failed institutions to fail (creative destruction)
- Retraining and labor market programs at sufficient scale

## Conclusion: The Credit System Is the Transmission Mechanism

The key insight from all historical bubble analysis is that **the credit system determines whether an asset bubble becomes an economic crisis**.

- Dot-com bust: Equity losses, minimal credit system damage → mild recession
- 2008 housing: Credit system nearly destroyed → severe global recession
- Japan 1990: Credit system damaged but hidden → 30-year stagnation
- Tulip mania: No credit system involvement → no recession

AI job displacement is an **employment event** that will become a **credit event** through the mortgage, auto, and consumer credit channels. The severity of the economic impact depends on how quickly credit losses materialize, whether the banking system can absorb them, and whether government response is adequate and timely.

The scenario described in `ai-job-displacement-scenario.md` (Stages 5–6) represents the credit transmission phase. Understanding this transmission is essential for positioning investment portfolios in advance of the critical stages.
